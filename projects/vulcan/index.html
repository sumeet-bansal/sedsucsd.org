About Vulcan-I

The Vulcan-I rocket was designed by the SEDS UCSD team in an attempt to be the first student team in the world to successfully fly and recover a rocket powered by a 3D printed engine.

The rocket is powered by the team's second 100% 3D printed engine named Ignus, designed to be a stepping stone for larger, more powerful engines. The rocket is liquid fueled in a blowdown system by liquid oxygen (LOx) and Jet-A propellant spanning 19 feet in length with a diameter of 8 inches.

On May 21st, 2016, SEDS UCSD became the first undergraduate organization in the world to succesfully launch a rocket powered by a 3D printed engine. Vulcan-I was launched in the Mojave Desert, at the Friends of Amateur Rocketry site.

Liftoff occurred at approximately 3:50pm and Vulcan-I soared to approximately 4000ft before returning to the surface.

Nosecone
Houses the electronics bay
View More Details
A printed circuit board, currently being debugged, was collectively designed by the Avionics team for the rocket’s avionics system. A pressure sensor, three-axis accelerometer, and GPS sensor were going to be utilized on the printed circuit board to ignite electronic matches within ejection wells in order to deploy the parachutes. As a fail-safe, a PerfectFlite altimeter commonly used in rocketry for parachute deployment will be connected in parallel to our custom designed avionics. Using a weather balloon, offsets from the altimeter and a separate GPS instrumentation system were compared and analyzed.
 
 
 
Aeroshells
Composed of fiberglass with a reinforced paper core. Covers the pneumatic lines and plumbing.
View More Details
The aero shells fill in the gaps in between the recovery bay and LOx tank, the LOx tank and RP- 1 tank, and RP-1 tank to the engine. In between these lie the aero frame struts that are capable of withstanding 6500 lbs of force, as well as the solenoids and plumbing between the engines. They consist of cardboard core with 2 layers of fiberglass/epoxy composite mixtures. In order to assemble the aero shell along with the raceway and boat tail/fins, they were designed in half circle pieces where one half would be permanent while the other would have the capability of being removable/
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Struts
Separates the tanks and are bolted to custom end caps to absorb both tensile and compressive loading.
 
 
 
 
Pneumatic Valve
Actuates LOx and RP-1 fuel lines.
View More Details
The plumbing for the rocket is a combination of hard 1/4 inch nylon tubing and 1/2 inch Aluminum tubing. For the propellant lines, we used the aluminum tubing along with -8 AN fittings to allow us to move LOx and RP-1 from the tanks to the engine. The RP-1 and LOx tanks will be pressurized to 850 psi and 900 psi respectively using pressurizing system with helium through the 1/4 inch nylon pipe.
 
 
Engine
Additively manufactured 3D-Printed engine made of Inconel 718
View More Details
The engine is a completely additive manufactured rocket motor with minimal post processing, designed for 750 lbf of thrust with a chamber pressure of 400 psi. It consists entirely of Inconel 718 complete with printed -8 AN fittings. A regenerative film layer cools inner passageways and the combustion chamber for increased efficiency. The injector plate is additively manufactured, creating channels and chasms which could only be produced layer-by-layer using 3-D printing technologies. The trajectory and pressures at which the propellants enter the combustion chamber control the efficiency of the combustion.
Designed by SEDS UCSD
 
 
 
 
Recovery Bay
Comprised of a series of bulkheads housing aluminum U-bolts. Recovery section occupied by a 28 foot main chute and 10 foot drogue shoot.
View More Details
The main and drogue parachute compartment each contain two ejection wells, each with two ematches installed. Through complex wiring, complete redundancy is installed in both the systems and explosive charges. Wires carrying current for parachute ejection must bridge the gap between the airframe and shoulder of the nosecone, which must separate during parachute deployment. A connector tight enough to not experience loss of continuity due to vibrations or launch acceleration, but loose enough so that it doesn't impede the separation of the nose cone and the airframe during parachute deployment has been installed.
 
 
 
 
 
Liquid Oxygen (LOx) Tank
Located forward of the RP-1 tank. Welded heat treated to custom end caps.
View More Details
The tanks are fabricated using a long hollow cylinder of 6061 Aluminum and a solid block of Aluminum, which was cut into individual end caps. These caps were CNC'd on the surface to accommodate flanges for the struts to attach to. The bottom of the end caps were chamfered and the caps were butt welded to the aluminum cylinders, ultimately creating two separate tanks. Each tank was band-welded by welding a protruding band around each of the butt welds. The welds were then head treated and hydro tested to 900psi, higher than the 800psi and 750psi to be used.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Fins
Composed of lightweight fiberglass with foam core to decrease weight and increase stabilization. Boat-tail fins utilizes fiberglass and foam as well and houses fins.
View More Details
The fins are attached to the boat tail with four bolts. The boat tail hangs 0.5 inch further down than the engine to help protect it on impact. Each boat tail is lined with an insulated material made of aluminum foil and foam to deflect heat and prevent the boat tail from warping due to heat. The boat tail is not attached to the rocket structure itself, but to the engine, which is attached to the rocket structure. The boat tail is attached using 2 fences that ride along the bottom and top of the flange of the rocket engine. The boat tail is unable to rotate, locking the fins in place.
